Range Shift Wizard Mapping future climate-induced changes to bird, amphibian, and mammal distributions in the Western Hemisphere
The maps available as part of the Range Shift Wizard are based on projected changes in the areas that are likely to be climatically for individual species. Projections are derived from correlative bioclimatic models—also referred to as niche models—that relate a single species' distributions to recent climatic conditions. These models are then applied to projected future climatic conditions to determine what areas will likely be climatically suitable for a species in the future. All maps presented here were based on models that could accurately predict species current distributions when the models were applied to a test dataset that was not used in model building. Nonetheless, these models are likely to capture actual species' climatic requirements to different degrees. For example, these models are based on current species ranges and thus if a species has been extirpated from much of its historical range, these models will be unable to represent all of the climatic conditions in which a species can exist. Similarly, these models do not address interactions between species that may help define distributions, they do not address the ability of some species to evolve in the face of climate change, and they do not address the ability of species to disperse to newly suitable climates. Despite these limitations, these models provide a first approximation of how many species will likely respond to projected climatic changes. Thus, although they cannot give us an accurate picture of where a specific species will be in 50 years, they can give us an idea of how different species might respond to climate change and how faunas might be altered. For a detailed description of the study that produced these models please see: http://depts.washington.edu/landecol/PDFS/turnover.pdf
